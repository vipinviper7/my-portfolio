Recent Updates — Zoho Sprints

Renewal reminders were going out over email. Most people never opened them. This is the feed I built to fix that.

TL;DR

Sprints had no way to reach users inside the product, so renewal reminders and updates were going out over email where nobody saw them. I designed an in-app feed that put it all in one place. Pushed back on the “one flat list” brief so action items don’t look like stuff you can ignore. Shipped in 2 weeks to 50K+ users, with zero design changes after handoff.

Vipin — Product Designer Manager — Design Lead 2 Engineers — Frontend PM — Product
Impact
  • 50K+ users
  • 4 update types in one feed
  • 0 layout changes after handoff

Key Takeaways

  • The whole point was reach. Renewal notices were sitting in inboxes nobody opened. This put them where users actually look.
  • The brief wanted one flat list. I pushed back and got the okay to make action items look different from the stuff you can ignore.
  • Shipped exactly as designed. Only thing that changed was the icons, and that was during eng review.

The Problem

Sprints couldn’t really reach users inside the product. Everything went out over email — renewal reminders, community posts, promotions. And email open rates were what they were. People were missing renewal notices, which isn’t a UX nitpick, that’s money walking out the door.

So I sat with the PM and we worked out what belonged in the feed and what didn’t. Engineering told us early what the existing notification setup could and couldn’t do. That shaped the structure before I’d drawn a single frame.

What Made It Tricky

Building a notification panel is easy. That wasn’t the problem.

The problem was four different kinds of content sharing one space — announcements, renewal reminders, community updates, promotions. A renewal reminder needs you to do something today. A community post doesn’t. If they all look the same, people stop reading all of them. Including the one that costs them money to miss.

The brief said put it all in one flat list. I didn’t agree with that, and I said so early.

Early Exploration

Before opening Figma I just listed everything out on paper. How urgent is each type, how often it shows up, whether it needs the user to act. Wanted the hierarchy clear in my head before I started pushing pixels around.

That’s where the real tension showed up — a renewal reminder and a promo banner have nothing in common except they’d be sitting in the same panel. They couldn’t look the same.

How the Design Evolved

First versions — Cards with a lot of visual weight. On their own they looked good. But you open this panel a few times a day, and all that hierarchy got tiring fast. It was too much.

Where I pushed back — Brief said flat list. I said renewal reminders shouldn’t look like a community post — one needs you to act, the other doesn’t. Someone missing a renewal is a real cost. I put both versions side by side in mockups and the PM came around pretty quick.

Where I got it wrong — I burned two iterations building a custom card system. Looked great in isolation. My manager pulled me back and said it didn’t feel like Sprints. He was right. A panel like this should feel like it was always there, not like something a designer dropped in to show off.

What stuck — Notification-style items that match how Sprints already looks. Action stuff gets a CTA. Passive stuff doesn’t. The difference comes from colour, not heavy boxes.

Final Design

One feed. Everything in one place — both for the teams sending updates and the users checking what needs them.

  • One stream, no tabs. We talked about splitting by type. Decided against it — tabs would just split attention. You should know what’s waiting in one look.
  • Colour does the sorting. A coloured indicator tells you what needs action vs. what’s just info. You don’t have to read everything to find the one that matters.
  • Short by default, expand if you want. Title and a line. Tap for the rest.
  • CTAs only where they belong. Eng needed a clean place to hang the buttons. Keeping action items visually separate solved that at the same time.

After Shipping

Shipped the way I designed it. Nothing changed in the layout or the flow after handoff. The one thing that did change was the icons — I’d used placeholders, and we swapped them for Sprints’ own set during eng review. Caught it early because I stayed in the loop.

Marketing and product both use the feed now as their main way to reach users in-app. That’s the win — the product got a channel it just didn’t have before.

What I Took Away

Pick the fight you can back up. I pushed on the flat list because I could point at a real cost — people missing renewals. That’s what moved the PM. “It doesn’t feel right” wouldn’t have.

Fitting in beats standing out. My custom cards were good. They were also wrong for the product. A screen that feels foreign just makes people trust it less. Best case is nobody notices it’s new.

Stick around after handoff. The icon thing got caught in eng review, not after launch, because I was still paying attention. That last bit of involvement isn’t extra work. It’s what keeps the rest from breaking.
