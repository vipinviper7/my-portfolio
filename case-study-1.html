Key Takeaways

  • Designed a single update feed that handles product announcements, subscription reminders, community updates, and promotions

  • Shipped intact to production with minor icon adjustments during development

The Problem

Zoho Sprints had no way to talk to its users from inside the product. Product updates went out via email — most users never saw them. Subscription reminders, community posts, promotions — none of it reached users in-app.

50K users were missing updates that mattered, and we had no channel to reach them.

I took on the challenge of designing a unified in-product updates system. I started by working with the PM to map out every content type, its urgency, and its frequency. The engineering team flagged what was feasible within Sprints' existing notification infrastructure — which shaped how I structured the feed from day one.

What Made It Tricky

The challenge wasn't "design a notification panel." It was fitting very different types of content into one space.

Product announcements users need to know about. Subscription reminders that require action. Community updates that are nice-to-have. Promotions most people ignore. All competing for the same screen space, all arriving at different frequencies.

Show too much, users ignore everything. Show too little, they miss what matters.

Early Exploration

Before opening Figma, I mapped out content types on paper — product announcements, subscription reminders, community updates, and promotions. Each had different urgency levels, frequencies, and actions. I needed to understand the information architecture before touching pixels.

How the Design Evolved

~10 iterations over two weeks. Here's how my thinking shifted:

Early on — I designed structured cards with rich visual hierarchy. They looked polished, but they were overdesigned. Too heavy for something users would see multiple times a day.

Where I pushed back — The initial requirement was to show all update types in a single undifferentiated list. I argued that subscription reminders (which need user action) should be visually separated from passive updates (product news). A user missing a renewal notice is a business problem, not just a UX problem. I made a case with mockups showing both approaches, and the PM agreed to differentiate them.

Where I was wrong — I spent two iterations building a custom card system that looked great in isolation but broke Sprints' existing design language. My manager pulled me back, and he was right. The best version of this feature should feel like it had always been part of the product — not like a new app living inside the old one.

What landed — Notification-style items with clear titles and context. Simple enough to scan, detailed enough to be useful. Action-driven updates with CTAs, passive ones without.

Final Design

🔍 Asset Needed Annotated close-up of the final UI — callouts for: content type indicators, action buttons on subscription reminders, time grouping, expand/collapse behavior

A single update feed inside the product. One place for everything.

  • One stream, no tabs. Everything sorted by time. We debated tabs-by-type early on, but the PM and I agreed it would fragment attention — users should see everything in one scan.
  • Visual distinction by update type — colour-coded indicators let users tell at a glance what needs attention vs. what's informational.
  • Scannable by default, expandable for detail. Most updates show title + one-liner. Tap to expand for full context.
  • Action-driven updates (subscription reminders) visually separated from passive ones — because the engineering team needed a clear hook for CTA buttons.

After Shipping

Before — No channel
Asset Needed Sprints UI before the feature — no updates panel
After — Unified feed

The core design shipped intact. The only change during development was redrawing the icons to better match Sprints' existing icon style — flagged during the engineering review, which I participated in.

Before this feature, there was zero in-product communication. Product updates went out via email — most users never saw them. Now there's a persistent, scannable feed that the marketing and product teams actively use to reach users.

What I Took Away

Fight for the right distinctions. Pushing for visual separation between action-required and passive updates wasn't just a design choice — it was a product decision that protected the business. Not every design battle is worth fighting, but this one was.

Know when to follow the system. My off-brand iterations taught me that consistency isn't a constraint — it's a feature. Users don't want novelty in a utility panel. They want familiarity.

Speed kills preciousness. Ten iterations in two weeks. None of them were sacred. That's the only way to find the right answer — make a lot of wrong ones first.
