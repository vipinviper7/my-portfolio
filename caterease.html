CaterEase — Catering Discovery & Booking

From a personal frustration to a fully functional app

Product: CaterEase
Role: UX Researcher & Designer
Program: 10K Designers (Cohort 11)
Status: Shipped

Where It Started

I was trying to book a caterer for my son's birthday. It was a mess — phone calls, WhatsApp messages, no way to compare prices, no transparency on what I was actually paying for.

That frustration became the project. When I had to pick a problem space for my 10K Designers cohort, I already knew what I wanted to fix.

The Problem

Booking a caterer in India runs entirely on phone calls, WhatsApp, and word-of-mouth. There's no way to compare options side-by-side, no upfront pricing, no written agreements.

What that actually looks like:

  • You call 3–5 caterers and get different prices from each — sometimes different prices from the same caterer
  • Delivery, setup, and staff costs show up after you've already committed
  • Menu items get changed without notice because nothing is in writing
  • On event day, you have zero visibility on whether your food is on track
  • If the experience is bad, there's nowhere to report it

Research

I started with what I knew — my own experience booking caterers — and expanded from there. I studied people in my circle who regularly book catering: a budget-conscious parent planning family functions, a corporate admin handling office events, a professional who pays premium and expects quality.

I also dug into the market: existing platforms, competitor gaps, online reviews, community complaints, and pricing patterns across Chennai.

Three patterns kept showing up across every segment:

Trust is the biggest barrier. People want verified caterers, real photos, FSSAI certification, and reviews from actual customers — not curated testimonials.

Pricing is a black box. The same caterer quotes different prices to different people. Hidden charges for delivery, setup, and staff are the norm, not the exception.

There's no accountability. Once you pay the advance, you have no leverage. No tracking, no written agreement, no recourse if quality drops.

Personas

Three personas emerged from the research, each representing a different catering customer:

Arun — Budget-Conscious Parent
Age 38 · Govt. employee · Birthdays, family functions, poojas

Calls 4–5 caterers, negotiates hard, decides on price. His biggest frustration: hidden charges that show up after booking.

“I just want to know the full cost upfront.”
Priya — Corporate Event Admin
Age 30 · IT Admin Manager · Corporate lunches, team events

Needs to book fast with minimal back-and-forth and get proper invoices. Her worst experience: food arrived 30 minutes late to a client meeting with no way to track it.

“I need something I can book in 10 minutes and trust that it'll show up on time.”
Abishek — Quality-Seeking Professional
Age 34 · Software Engineer · Weddings, anniversaries

Researches extensively, willing to pay premium. Paid ₹40,000 advance once and got mediocre quality with no recourse.

“I paid premium but got average food. There should be a way to hold caterers accountable.”

Root Causes

Using the 5 Whys framework on each persona's top pain point, six root causes surfaced:

  • No digital agreement system — everything is verbal
  • No feedback loop — bad experiences go unreported
  • No real-time order or delivery tracking
  • No locked order summary — menu items change after booking
  • No transparent cost breakdown — hidden charges everywhere
  • No payment tracking — unclear advance/balance schedule

Every root cause pointed to the same gap: there's no system. The entire catering industry runs on trust and phone calls. The app needed to be that system.

Building It

After the research and design, I wanted to see if this could actually work as a product — not just as a Figma file. I used Claude Code to vibe-code a fully functional React Native app.

  • 26 screens, 12 demo caterers, 114 menu items
  • Built with React Native (Expo), Supabase backend, NativeWind
  • Fully navigable demo — browse caterers, build orders, track bookings

The goal wasn't production-ready code. It was proving that the research held up when you could actually tap through the experience.

What I Took Away

Start with a problem you've felt. The best part of my research came from already understanding the frustration firsthand. It made every insight sharper because I wasn't discovering the problem — I was confirming and expanding what I already knew.

Research is the real design work. The screens came together fast because the decisions were already made. Every layout choice traced back to a finding. That's what I want my design process to look like going forward.

Building it changed how I think about design. Vibe-coding the app forced me to confront things Figma doesn't — edge cases, empty states, real data. It made the design better retroactively.
